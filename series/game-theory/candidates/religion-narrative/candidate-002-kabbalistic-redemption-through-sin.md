## Extracted Passage (GT28)

> The idea of sin is you must break yourself before you can learn the wisdom to welcome God into your heart. The story that explains this idea is that of King David. King David was the greatest ruler of the Israelites. And one day he falls in love with a woman named Bathsheba... David kills Uriah the Hittite... God kills his son and this forces David into mourning. When he mourns, he breaks his heart. When he breaks his heart, he welcomes God back into his soul... The Israelites remember David not as a great conqueror, not as a great king, but as a man who understood his humility... Nothing matters except your relationship with God. The whole world could be in nuclear war. Doesn't matter. What matters is your true faith in God. So from these three things, we now understand what's going on in Gaza and the Middle East where the Israelis think that hey, if I break the world and I force the entire world to hate me and I sin so much that I suffer, that's a good thing because that's going to let me develop a close relationship with God and this will repair the world.

## Analysis

The lecturer maps the Kabbalistic concept of *Tikkun Olam* (repairing the world) onto a radical geopolitical strategy: **destruction as precondition for redemption.** Drawing on the Lurianic Kabbalah tradition (via Gershom Scholem's "Redemption Through Sin"), this candidate reveals how a religious narrative — the David-Bathsheba story — is weaponized as a geopolitical rationale. The Jerusalem theocratic faction views the destruction of Tel Aviv not as tragedy but as spiritual purification: the animal soul (Tel Aviv, secularism, materialism) must be destroyed so the divine soul (Jerusalem, faith, submission to God) can rise. This narrative structure directly explains why some Israeli religious leaders "are not at all saddened" by Iranian missiles striking Tel Aviv — they see it as accelerating messianic redemption.
