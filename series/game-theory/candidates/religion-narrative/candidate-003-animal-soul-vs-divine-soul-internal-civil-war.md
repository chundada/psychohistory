# Candidate 003: Animal Soul vs. Divine Soul — Internal Civil War as Religious Conflict

**Source:** GT14-The-Law-of-Proximity.md, lines 242–298  
**Signal Tags:** divine, animal soul, Jerusalem, Tel Aviv, redemption, repentance

## Extracted Passage

> Tel Aviv is democratic. Jerusalem is a theocracy. Tel Aviv is open, cosmopolitan. Jerusalem is very religious, very conservative. They hate each other. The fact that Tel Aviv is getting destroyed right now in this war, people in Jerusalem are actually okay with this. They're not like, "Oh my god, they're killing Israelis." No, no, no. Tel Aviv is the great Satan. Let's just destroy it so that we can build a theocracy... They believe that in every person there's a divine soul that comes from God, but it is trapped in the animal soul. Tel Aviv is the animal soul. Jerusalem is the divine soul. The animal soul seeks material comfort. You want to have sex. You want to get drunk. You want to drive a Ferrari. But inside of you is the divine soul that only seeks to be with God. Tel Aviv represents the animal soul of Israel. Jerusalem represents the divine soul of Israel. So it's okay if Iran is destroying Tel Aviv. Good.

## Analysis

This candidate demonstrates how **religious dualism (body vs. soul, material vs. spiritual) is mapped onto urban geography and political factionalism.** The lecturer literalizes the Gnostic/Kabbalistic concept of the soul's division: the animal soul (*nefesh behamit*) versus the divine soul (*nefesh Elohit*). This is not presented as theology but as an active political force determining war strategy. The Jerusalem faction's belief that "destruction is good because it purifies" explains a seemingly irrational geopolitical behavior: why some Israelis welcome their own country's destruction. This is a critical religious-narrative insight: when a society internalizes a theological dualism, external aggression can be reframed as spiritual cleansing. The Law of Proximity (the game closest to you determines your decisions) shows that Israel's internal religious civil war is actually more determinative than the external war with Iran.
